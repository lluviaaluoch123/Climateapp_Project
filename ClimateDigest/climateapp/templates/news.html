National Mitigation Measures & Low-Carbon Growth

Kenya is globally recognized for its low-carbon development path, with its strategy focused on enhancing carbon sinks and expanding its already dominant renewable energy sector. Key pillars include:

  • Renewable Energy Leadership

    Over 90% of Kenya's grid electricity is already from green sources, primarily Geothermal, Hydro, and Wind e.g., Lake Turkana Wind Power. The goal is to reach 100% clean energy generation in the near future and utilize the vast geothermal potential (up to 10,000MW).

    Source: Ministry of Energy / EPRA

  • 15 Billion Tree Initiative

    The government is driving a national campaign to grow 15 billion trees by 2032 to increase the national tree cover to over 30%. This is a massive Nature-Based Solution (NBS) aimed at both carbon sequestration and enhanced water security.

    Source: Ministry of Environment and Forestry

  • Green Mobility Transition

    There is significant push and investment towards Electric Vehicles, especially in the two- and the Boda Boda sector, which is critical for last-mile transport. Tax incentives and local assembly plants are driving this clean transition in the transport sector.

The Dual Threat: Droughts, Floods & Lake Swelling

Kenya is highly vulnerable, with its economy heavily dependent on climate-sensitive sectors like agriculture. The impacts are characterized by a vicious cycle of prolonged droughts followed by severe, unseasonal flooding.

  • Recurrent Drought Crises

    The recent Horn of Africa drought (2020-2023) was the worst in 40 years, severely impacting over 5 million people in Arid and Semi-Arid Lands (ASALs) like Turkana, Garissa, and Marsabit. It decimated livestock and forced pastoralists to migrate, leading to resource conflicts.

  • Intense El Nino Flooding

    Following the drought, the 2023/2024 El NiÃ±o rains caused catastrophic riverine and flash flooding, displacing hundreds of thousands, damaging critical infrastructure, and destroying farmlands in counties like Tana River, Mandera, and Nairobi.

  • Rising Lake Waters & Coastal Threats

    Several Rift Valley lakes like lake Baringo and Turkana have swelled dramatically, submerging homes, schools, and health facilities. On the coast, sea-level rise threatens infrastructure and freshwater aquifers in low-lying areas of Mombasa and Lamu.

    Source: UNEP / World Bank

Locally-Led Technology & Adaptation Solutions

Kenyan innovators are leveraging digital tools and local knowledge to build resilience, especially in food security and disaster preparedness.

  • Climate-Smart Agriculture (CSA)

    Adoption of technologies like solar-powered drip irrigation and rainwater harvesting systems lined water pans is enabling farmers to transition from unreliable rainfed agriculture to high-value horticulture, even in ASALs.

  • Digital Climate Advisories (SMS/App)

    Platforms are integrating high-resolution satellite and meteorological data like th e Kenya Agricultural Observatory Platform to deliver tailored SMS advisories to hundreds of thousands of farmers and pastoralists on optimal planting times, pest control, and livestock movement.

  • Carbon Markets Framework

    Kenya's new Climate Change Act of 2023 is establishing robust legal and policy guidelines for participation in voluntary carbon markets. This aims to channel private climate finance into community-based projects like afforestation and clean cookstoves.

Policy Milestones & Global Leadership

Kenya continues to drive the African climate agenda, hosting major international policy forums and consolidating national commitments.

  • Second Nationally Determined Contribution Kenya submitted its updated NDC in 2025, committing to a 35% reduction in GHG emissions by 2035 relative to the Business As Usual scenario, requiring significant international investment.
  • Africa Climate Summit Following the successful hosting of the inaugural ACS in Nairobi (2023), Kenya is focused on implementing the Nairobi Declaration, pushing for climate finance reform and scaling up clean energy investment across the continent.
  • UNEP Headquarters: As the host of the UN Environment Programme (UNEP) in Nairobi, Kenya remains a critical diplomatic center for global environmental policy, as seen during events like the UN Environment Assembly (UNEA-7).
